For as long as teams have built products, a job title named a contribution. **Engineer** meant the person who produced the code. **Designer** meant the one who produced the design. **QA** meant the one who produced the checks. Title equaled contribution because **production was scarce** — a human had the focus and capacity to be good at producing exactly one kind of thing.

AI breaks that equation. When generation becomes abundant, producing the artifact stops being the scarce, defining act — and the limit that confined a person to a single contribution dissolves. One human can now span several kinds of contribution, because an AI **delegate** extends their reach past their own focus and capacity.

So the unit of a team is no longer the title. It is the **direction** a person sets right now — a **motive** (what they want from the work) pursued from their **angle** of expertise — fulfilled by them wielding a delegate they direct to act on their behalf. Motive alone is half the story: the angle is what tells them *which* want is worth holding and *how* to judge against it.

<Aside type="tip" title="Reading the diagrams">
A shared visual language runs through every diagram on these pages: **actors** (humans) have a solid accent border, **delegates** (agents) a dashed border — *capacity, wielded* — a **gate** an amber border, and an **artifact** (a surface, product, or piece of work) a teal border.
</Aside>

That is the whole model in one move. The rest of these pages unpack it.

## The shift in one table

| | Production scarce (pre-AI) | Production abundant (with AI) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Unit of a team | the **title** — fixed to one contribution | the **direction** — a motive, from your angle of expertise |
| A person | does one kind of thing well | spans several, via delegates |
| Producing the artifact | the scarce, defining act | cheap; no longer the bottleneck |
| The scarce thing | making the thing | deciding *what* to make, and judging it |

<Aside type="caution" title="Abundance is a dial, not a switch">
The model is **abundance-relative**, not a claim that everything is now cheap. Where generation is cheap (common code, tests, boilerplate) one human spans many motives. Where it stays expensive (novel, research-grade work) the old `title = contribution` rule survives locally. The model degrades gracefully — same parts, less compression.
</Aside>

## Why bother getting this right

Get the motives right and everything downstream follows — what each person needs to do, how the work plays out, what surfaces they hand to their delegates. Get them wrong — by treating an AI as a teammate with its own goals, or by organizing the team on a timeline instead of by motive — and the work overlaps, the hand-offs blur, and the confusion compounds.

The model has one load-bearing rule, stated up front so nothing later contradicts it: **an AI is never an actor.** A human holds the motive and the accountability; the AI is capacity the human wields. The next page shows where that line falls in work you already do.
